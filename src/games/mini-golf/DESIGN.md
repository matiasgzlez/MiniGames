# Storybook Fairway — Golden Hour

A design philosophy for a world drawn in ink and bathed in late-afternoon light. Written as the art direction for this game's Three.js renderer; every visual decision in the course, the ball, the props and the sky answers to it. This document supersedes the first "flat noon" draft: the target ambience is the game's key art (`docs/hole in none.png`) — an overgrown garden minigolf at golden hour — translated into the engine's cel-shaded language.

The course is a toy diorama abandoned in an enchanted garden. Every hole is a floating island of mossy earth, and the garden has been growing over it for years: walls of weathered honey wood wear a cap of moss, barrels and lantern posts lean where a groundskeeper left them, and an old stone windmill turns slowly in the distance. The player looks down on it like a child who found the diorama at the bottom of a grandmother's garden — familiar, warm, slightly wild. Nothing pretends to be real: edges carry a dark ink outline, light falls in flat painted bands, and geometry stays chunky and rounded. If a prop would not survive being drawn with a brush in three strokes, it is too complicated for this world.

Light is a single low sun, and the hour is always golden. One warm amber key light plus a honeyed hemisphere, rendered through a hard-stepped toon ramp so every surface resolves into two or three flat bands — never gradients. Shadow is a color decision: the shaded band of the grass is a deeper, mossier green, never gray. The sky is a painted gradient — cream-gold at the horizon climbing into deep dusk blue — and a soft warm haze eats the distance, so the far foliage melts into light the way the key art's waterfall garden does. The lanterns answer the sun with small amber pools; they are the only lights the player adds to the scene. Bloom and tone mapping are still refused — the cartoon wants crisp, not glowy — and the ink outline stays thin, dark and constant.

Color is a small painted vocabulary, and each hue has a job. Mossy grass green is the ground truth, split into fairway and putting tones. Weathered honey wood is the architecture, always capped with moss so every wall reads overgrown. Danger and moving things speak in poppy red with cream trim — the striped barber pole at the cup, the windmill bar, the bumpers; the shortcut ramps speak once in sky blue so the risky line is always findable. The ball is the only pure white object in the scene. Around and below the dioramas, deep foliage greens ring the world so the camera never lands on empty sky: the garden is the page this book is drawn on.

Form is toy-proportioned and honestly repeated: planks, stripes, dimples, hoops, blades. Props are characters — a barrel is fat with iron hoops, a lantern hangs at a jaunty angle, the mill's blades are timber crosses — and each one is placed where a model-maker would put it, guarding a corner or watching a green, never scattered at random. The composition is played, not only looked at, so hierarchy is absolute: white ball, red flag, red hazards, blue shortcut, warm lantern accents, everything else quiet green and wood. Every band of the ramp, every outline and every hue is tuned until the frame looks like a meticulously crafted picture-book plate at sunset — the product of countless refinements, with nothing left that a master illustrator would still be tempted to touch.
